## Overview

Remote viewing is the claimed practice of obtaining impressions of remote or hidden targets using only the mind. The term was introduced in 1971 by physicists Russell Targ and Harold Puthoff at the Stanford Research Institute (SRI) in Menlo Park, California, in collaboration with the artist and self-described psychic Ingo Swann. From 1972, SRI received funding from the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) and later the Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA) to investigate possible intelligence applications. The classified programme passed under several codenames — Gondola Wish, Grill Flame, Center Lane, Sun Streak, and finally Stargate Project — and ran until its closure in 1995, when a CIA-commissioned evaluation by the American Institutes for Research (AIR), with statistician Jessica Utts and psychologist Ray Hyman, concluded that remote viewing had not produced actionable intelligence and that earlier positive results were attributable to methodological flaws. The mainstream scientific community treats remote viewing as pseudoscience.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Coined in December 1971 at the American Society for Psychical Research, New York, by Russell Targ, Harold Puthoff, and Ingo Swann to describe a structured experimental protocol distinct from older parapsychological terms such as 'travelling clairvoyance' or 'astral projection'.
- **Meaning**: 'Remote' (at a distance from the subject) and 'viewing' (perceiving) — perceiving a target at distance.
- **First use**: 1971; first appears in print in early SRI reports and in Targ and Puthoff's 1974 Nature paper 'Information transmission under conditions of sensory shielding'.

## Notable Sightings

### Pat Price 'Soviet site' / Semipalatinsk-style trial ⭐
> **Date**: 1973-01-01  
> **Location**: Stanford Research Institute, Menlo Park, California (claimed remote target in the Soviet Union)  
> **Witnesses**: 3 (SRI researcher (Russell Targ), SRI researcher (Harold Puthoff), Subject (Pat Price))  
In a series of CIA-funded trials at SRI, former police officer Pat Price produced sketches and descriptions of an alleged Soviet site that proponents said matched features of the target. The case is heavily cited by RV proponents. Independent reviewers have noted that hits were selected post hoc and that target-pool issues complicate scoring.
**Credibility**: low — A foundational anecdote of the SRI programme, but not regarded by mainstream evaluators as evidence of RV.
**Sources**: Targ, R. & Puthoff, H. (1977), Mind-Reach; Mumford, M. D., Rose, A. M. & Goslin, D. A. (1995), AIR Report

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Anomalous cognition / psi hypothesis** _(Russell Targ, Harold Puthoff, Edwin May, Jessica Utts (statistical reading))_

Remote viewing reflects a real (if weak and statistically diffuse) human capacity for non-sensory information acquisition, potentially physical in nature but currently unaccounted for by mainstream physics.

### Skeptical

**Methodological artefact / sensory cueing** _(David Marks, Richard Kammann, Ray Hyman, James Randi)_

Apparent successes in SRI trials are attributable to cueing in transcripts (e.g. references to earlier sites), inadequate target-pool design, and post hoc judging rather than to a paranormal effect.

**No actionable intelligence value** _(American Institutes for Research, CIA leadership in 1995)_

Whatever the academic statistical questions, the operational programmes produced no intelligence of demonstrated value, and were terminated for that reason.

**Pseudoscience classification** _(Mainstream scientific consensus)_

Remote viewing fails standard demarcation criteria for science: there is no specified mechanism, no robust replication, and no progression of theory.
